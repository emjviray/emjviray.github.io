Project Description:

Developed a full machine learning pipeline to redesign NBA court geometry by optimizing three-point efficiency and restoring offensive diversity. Leveraged NBA shot-tracking data to model player shot behavior and scoring outcomes across variable court dimensions (3PT radius and baseline width). Implemented Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) to generate spatial shot distributions and simulate realistic shot locations, and trained a Feedforward Neural Network (MLP) to learn nonlinear relationships between court geometry and expected scoring output. Used XGBoost (gradient boosted trees) to predict shot-level make probabilities based on location, player tendencies, and court parameters, and integrated a fatigue-adjusted Logistic Regression model to account for time-dependent performance decline.
Central to the system is the Expected Points Per Game (EPPG) equation, which models scoring as a function of shot distribution and success probabilities:
- a(r,w), b(r,w): proportions of 2PT vs 3PT attempts under court geometry
- p2(r,w), p3(r,w): probability of making 2PT and 3PT shots
- Combined into expected scoring weighted by shot value (2 vs 3 points)
This probabilistic framework is used within a Monte Carlo simulation to generate full games (possessions, shot selection, outcomes), producing realistic scoring distributions across teams. An exhaustive grid search optimizer evaluates 200+ court configurations to identify the optimal geometry maximizing team three-point efficiency while maintaining balanced scoring (~200 total points per game).
Result: Discovered an AI-optimized NBA court with extended three-point distance and increased width that significantly improves shooting efficiency and strategic shot selection.
